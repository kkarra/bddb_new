Broad Domains constrain transcriptional variability at key cell identity and function genes


Berenice A. Benayoun*1,Elizabeth A. Pollina*1,2, Duygu Ucar*1, Edith Wong1, Elena Mancini1, Salah Mahmoudi1, Benjamin Hitz1, Keerthana Devarajan1, Rhaki Gupta1,Thomas A. Rando3, Julie C. Baker1, Michael P. Snyder1, J. Michael Cherry1, and Anne Brunet1,2,3

*These authors contributed equally to this work.
1Department of Genetics, Stanford University School of Medicine, Stanford, CA 94306, USA
2Cancer Biology Program, Stanford University, Stanford CA 94305, USA
3Glenn Laboratories for the Biology of Aging at Stanford University, Stanford CA94305,USA

SUMMARY


Trimethylation of Histone H3 at Lysine 4 (H3K4me3) is a chromatin modification traditionally known to mark the transcription start sites of active genes. Here we identify another ‘flavor’ of H3K4me3 deposition that spreads broadly over genes and is present in all cells and species tested. Broad H3K4me3 domains are underlied by a complex epigenomic signature for which breadth is a simple proxy. Broad H3K4me3 domains are remodeled upon cell identity changes and preferentially mark genes that are essential for identity and function of a given cell. Experiments in neural stem/progenitor cells confirm that broad H3K4me3 domains can be used as a discovery tool to identify new regulators of these cells. This chromatin signature differs from other signatures associated with cellspecific transcription. Remarkably, genes marked by broad H3K4me3 domains have reduced transcriptional variability. Thus, broad H3K4me3 domains may serve to ensure the transcriptional robustness of critical cell identity genes, and we have named them ‘Buffer Domains’.
